# Automotive Basics: Engine, Power Train & Drive Train — Bosch
# L01 - Automotive Power Train & Drive Train: From Engine to Wheels
Duration: ~45 min | Slides: 1-49 (49 slides)

=== NARRATION SCRIPT ===

[INTRO]
[Slide 1] Every time a car accelerates onto a highway, a precisely engineered chain of systems converts fuel into motion in milliseconds. [Slide 2] This lesson follows the Bosch Automotive Basics framework — covering vehicle structure, powertrain, drivetrain, chassis, body, braking, and electronics. [Slide 3] By the end, you will identify every major system in a modern vehicle and explain how they interact to move a car safely from engine to wheels.

[WHY]
[Slide 4] Bosch organizes its automotive work under Mobility Solutions — home to divisions like Powertrain Solutions and Chassis Systems Control. [Slide 5] Vehicles fall into three categories: Passenger Cars (PC), Commercial Vehicles (CV), and Off-Highway Vehicles (OHW). Each category demands different powertrain and drivetrain configurations — understanding the distinctions is foundational for any automotive engineer or technician. [Slide 6] A vehicle is built from five major system groups: Body Frame, Power Train, Chassis Systems, Electrical and Electronics Systems, and Car Multimedia. Miss any one of these, and the vehicle cannot function safely.

[WHAT]
[Slide 7] Power Train equals Engine plus Drive Train. That single equation defines this entire lesson. [Slide 8] Visualize it on an Audi Q7 with quattro all-wheel drive: the engine generates torque; the drive train distributes that torque to the wheels. Together they form the Power Train. [Slide 9] The Engine is the power source — it converts fuel into torque. Engines are classified by combustion type (internal vs. external), cylinder arrangement (In-line, V-Engine, Radial, Opposed), operating concept (2-stroke, 4-stroke, Wankel rotary, Stirling, Gas turbine), and fuel type. [Slide 10] The four most common arrangements in production vehicles are the In-line engine, V-engine, Radial engine, and Boxer engine. [Slide 11] Inside every internal combustion engine, key components work together: the crankshaft, connecting rod, pistons and piston rings, cylinder block, cylinder head, camshaft, intake and exhaust valves, combustion chamber, and spark plug or glow plug. [Slide 12] Operation follows either a 2-stroke cycle — induction and combustion combined in two piston movements — or the 4-stroke cycle: Induction, Compression, Power (Ignition), and Exhaust. [Slide 13] Fuel delivery splits into two strategies. Direct Injection sprays fuel directly into the combustion chamber for both gasoline and diesel engines, improving efficiency. [Slide 14] Indirect Injection — Port Fuel Injection for gasoline, Swirl Chamber injection for diesel — mixes fuel before it enters the cylinder.

[HOW]
[Slide 15] Between the engine and gearbox sit the Coupling Devices. A mechanical Clutch has eleven key components: crankshaft, flywheel, clutch disc, pressure plate, diaphragm spring, input shaft, release bearing, clutch cover, diaphragm spring fulcrum ring, locating pin, and rivet. A dual-mass flywheel adds vibration damping. [Slide 16] Automatic transmissions replace the clutch with a Torque Converter — an impeller driven by the engine creates fluid flow, a stator multiplies torque, and a lock-up clutch with damper engages at cruise speed for efficiency. [Slide 17] The Drive Train has five components: Gearbox, Propeller Shaft, Drive Shaft (left), Drive Shaft (right), and Differential. Every one of these must work in sequence for torque to reach the wheels. [Slide 18] The Gearbox comes in four transmission types: Manual Transmission (MT) uses a clutch housing, input shaft, output shaft, synchronizers, and a countershaft; Automatic Transmission (AT); Continuously Variable Transmission (CVT); and Automated Manual Transmission (AMT). [Slide 19] Each type has a distinct gearshift pattern — the MT H-gate, CVT with no fixed steps, the AT P-R-N-D-L selector, and the AMT paddle or sequential lever. [Slide 20] The Differential balances wheel speed in corners using a Ring Gear, Pinion Gear, Spider Gears, Side Gears, and left and right axle shafts.

[FLOW]
[Slide 21] Chassis systems begin with Steering. Mechanical steering uses a rack-and-pinion gear with constant velocity joints and a subframe-mounted pinion. [Slide 22] Hydraulic Power Steering adds a pump, fluid reservoir, rotary pressure control valve, fluid lines, and a hydraulic gear piston to reduce driver effort. [Slide 23] Electric Power Steering replaces hydraulic components with a torque sensor, vehicle-speed input, ECU, and an assist motor — lighter, more fuel-efficient, and software-tunable. [Slide 24] Front suspension integrates MacPherson struts, coil springs, ball joints, CV drive shafts and boots, and tie rod ends into a unified corner structure. [Slide 25] Rear suspension for commercial and body-on-frame vehicles commonly uses leaf springs with spring eyes, bushes, and an anti-roll bar mounted to the axle casing. [Slide 26] The Vehicle Frame and Body serves four roles: aerodynamics and aeroacoustics management, occupant safety and crash protection, structural mounting for all other systems, and housing for lights, seats, windshields, doors, windows, and HVAC. [Slide 27] Body construction uses two philosophies: Uni-Body (unibody monocoque) common in passenger cars, and Body-on-Frame used in trucks and off-highway vehicles — each with different materials including cold-formed steel, hot-formed steel, aluminium castings, and aluminium extrusions.

[PRACTICE]
[Slide 28] The Braking System overview includes every sub-component: vacuum power booster, master cylinder, brake fluid reservoir, brake lines and hoses, disc rotors with calipers and brake pads, drum brakes with wheel cylinders, brake shoes and hold-down springs, parking brake cables and handle, combination valve, and bleed valves. [Slide 29] Power assistance comes from two technologies: the Vacuum Brake Booster uses engine vacuum to multiply pedal force; the iBooster is an electromechanical unit providing situation-dependent assist — critical for hybrid and electric vehicles with no engine vacuum. [Slide 30] Disc brakes clamp friction pads against a rotor attached to the wheel; drum brakes press shoes against the inside of a drum. [Slide 31] Amplification is dramatic: a 250 N driver pedal force becomes 1000 N after the booster, then 4200 N at the caliper piston, and finally 18,530 N of brake force at the disc. [Slide 32] Parking brakes exist in two forms: a traditional handbrake with cable, and an Automatic Parking Brake using a brushless electric motor, gearbox, and control electronics integrated into the caliper. [Slide 33] Car Multimedia systems include the Head Unit, Infotainment, Truck Radio, Coach Entertainment, Electronic Horizon, Digital Instrumentation, Digital Mirror Camera System, Driver Monitoring, and Connectivity — all networked into the vehicle. [Slide 34] The Gasoline Engine Management System ECU manages injectors, ignition coils, spark plugs, camshaft phasers, throttle device, knock sensors, oxygen sensors, air mass meter, exhaust temperature sensors, and carbon canister purge. [Slide 35] The Diesel Engine Management System adds a high-pressure fuel pump, rail pressure sensor, pressure-limiting valve, variable-geometry turbo, EGR throttle plate, NOx sensor, and the Denoxtronic SCR AdBlue dosing module. [Slide 36] Active Safety combines three systems — ABS, TCS, and ESP — sharing a hydraulic unit with ECU, wheel-speed sensors, steering angle sensor, and yaw-rate and lateral acceleration sensors. [Slide 37] ABS prevents wheel lock under heavy braking by modulating pressure wheel by wheel, reducing stopping distance while maintaining steerability. [Slide 38] TCS intervenes when wheel slip exceeds traction limits during acceleration, cutting engine torque or applying individual wheel brakes. [Slide 39] ESP extends both by actively stabilizing the vehicle in all driving situations — reducing skid risk and maintaining maneuverability in extreme conditions. [Slide 40] ESP corrects two instability modes: understeering (front-end pushes wide — yaw rate too small) and oversteering (rear breaks away — yaw rate too large). [Slide 41] Passive Safety centers on the Airbag system: a Crash Sensor triggers the Igniter, Sodium Azide generates Nitrogen Gas to inflate the bag through filters, all governed by the Airbag ECU. Sensor types include UFS Upfront Sensor, RCS Rear Crash Sensor, PAS Peripheral Acceleration Sensor, PPS, and PCS Pedestrian Pressure Sensor. [Slide 42] ADAS delivers safety, comfort, and driver support across six function domains: Emergency Braking, Lane Keeping and Changing, Evasion, Travelling distance and speed control, Turning and Crossing assistance, and Pedestrian Protection — with the driver still fully responsible. [Slide 43] SAE defines six automation levels 0 through 5. Level 0 is no automation; Level 2 provides highway pilot and urban assist with driver monitoring; Level 3 adds sensor-data fusion with map data; Level 4 enables urban automated taxi operations; Level 5 is full automation in all conditions. [Slide 44] ADAS hardware includes Front Radar (base, plus, premium), Multi-purpose Video Camera, Lidar, Ultrasonic sensors for park assist, Surround View cameras, and Rear/Positioning sensors — each mapped to specific functions like Predictive Emergency Braking, Lane Assist, Park and Maneuver Assist, and DASy Light and Sight Assistance. [Slide 45] Adaptive Cruise Control (ACC, including Stop and Go) measures time gap to the preceding vehicle and controls speed and distance automatically, handling cut-in and cut-out scenarios. [Slide 46] Lane Keeping Support (LKS) tracks vehicle position within the lane, detects unintentional departure, and applies smooth steering or brake intervention to return the car to lane center. [Slide 47] Traffic Jam Assist automates longitudinal and lateral guidance from 0 to 60 km/h on highways, with hands-off speed-dependent time limitation — driver still monitors. [Slide 48] Automatic and Remote Park Assist (APA/RPA) handles full parking slot entry and exit including longitudinal control, lateral control, and gear selection — activated, monitored, and deactivated by the driver from inside or outside the vehicle.

[SUMMARY]
[Slide 49] Power Train equals Engine plus Drive Train — that equation ties together every system covered today. The Engine converts fuel to torque; Coupling Devices transfer it smoothly; the Gearbox, Propeller Shaft, Drive Shafts, and Differential deliver it to the wheels. Chassis systems — steering and suspension — keep the vehicle on course; the Body protects and supports; Braking systems stop it safely; and Electronics from ABS to Level 4 ADAS ensure the whole system remains intelligent and safe. Master these relationships, and you hold the complete map of a modern vehicle.
